The Science In Plain English

Every Company Has a Filter. ISC Measures It.

Think of a healthy company like a car engine. When something goes wrong — fuel prices spike, a competitor undercuts — the healthy company absorbs it and corrects. Margins dip but recover. The system self-corrects.

A company in structural decline does the opposite. Each shock amplifies the next. Revenue recovery doesn't fix margins. Interest costs compound. The system amplifies rather than corrects. ISC measures which direction a company is going — before the financial statements fully reflect it.

Why ISC Financial Is Better

Most financial tools measure the state of a company — what the numbers say right now. ISC measures the dynamics — whether the financial system is moving toward stability or away from it. That's a fundamentally different measurement, and it shows up earlier.

The ISC coupling score (C) measures the correlation between rolling variance and rolling autocorrelation in a company's financial time series. When that correlation is strongly negative, the company is self-correcting — variance spikes get damped out. When it turns positive, variance and persistence rise together — the amplifying pattern that precedes structural collapse.

This measurement has AUC=0.96 on 45 historical financial failure cases — meaning it correctly classified structural health in 96% of cases. It leads Altman Z by 1–5 quarters in deteriorating companies.
